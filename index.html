Application for Masters Position

Dear Professor. Bezemer,

I want to step out of my professional career as a ML/Software Engineer, with over five years building intelligent systems, to pursue graduate research. The University of Alberta is where I want to do it, and I would be very interested in joining your research lab to take that experience from industry instinct to grounded research.

My academic journey has been a continuous exploration of how intelligent systems learn, adapt, and defend themselves against uncertainty and adversarial threats. I hold a master's degree in Big Data Technologies, where my dissertation focused on adversarial robustness in machine learning using ESRGAN. This work strengthened my technical foundation in deep learning while deepening my interest in AI security, adversarial learning, and privacy-preserving machine learning. Through this research, I developed a strong appreciation for how models can learn rich, transferable representations from data while remaining resilient to attacks.

Independent Research

More recently, I conducted independent research in two areas. The first was Voice Command Fingerprinting on Smart Home Speakers, investigating how acoustic and behavioural patterns in voice interactions can be leveraged to identify and profile users, even in the absence of explicit identifying information. The second was Web Fingerprinting, where I explored how browser-specific behavioural signals can be used to track and identify users across sessions without relying on traditional identifiers. Both lines of work exposed critical privacy vulnerabilities in widely deployed systems and reinforced my interest in the intersection of machine learning, side-channel analysis, and security. They also deepened my appreciation for the broader challenge of building intelligent systems that are not only effective but trustworthy and privacy aware. Both lines of work are available at https://github.com/opssondesigns/Voice-Command-Fingerprinting

Research Alignment

I came across your paper "XBIDetective: Leveraging Vision Language Models for Identifying Cross-Browser Visual Inconsistencies" on your lab's website and found the approach genuinely interesting, particularly the three-stage pipeline that filters out advertisements and dynamic elements before flagging true inconsistencies. That design decision stood out to me because it mirrors a challenge I have run into in my own work on adversarial robustness and web fingerprinting, the problem of separating meaningful signal from expected variation. In web fingerprinting specifically, I have worked on identifying browser-specific behavioral patterns, which shares a structural similarity with what XBIDetective is doing. My software engineering background also gives me an appreciation for the deployment challenges you describe, like bot detection interfering with Selenium and the difficulty of capturing consistent screenshots across browsers at scale.

The deeper question that draws me to this work is how we build systems that remain reliable in environments that are inherently inconsistent. My experience in adversarial robustness has reinforced that the hardest problems are rarely about catching obvious failures. They are about knowing when variation is expected and when it is not. I am curious about where VLM-based detection pipelines become brittle at scale and how they might be hardened against those edge cases.

Professional Journey

My professional journey has centred on building secured software systems for healthcare applications. My current role focuses on designing intelligent systems that process large volumes of sensitive data efficiently while emphasizing security, data integrity, and system resilience. Working at the intersection of machine learning and software engineering has revealed the limitations of conventional models when faced with cybersecurity threats and has strengthened my interest in robust system design and secure ML systems that operate reliably under uncertainty.

As my academic and professional experiences converged, my research interests evolved toward the intersection of secure software/IoT systems and AI/ML. I am motivated by the challenge of building intelligent systems that can reason about code semantics, system behaviour, and adversarial intent. My interests include applying large language models to secure code analysis, vulnerability detection, and automated reasoning about software/IoT dependencies, as well as leveraging representation learning to identify anomalous or malicious patterns across multimodal system data.

I believe this applied, security-focused perspective will contribute a strong and diverse viewpoint to interdisciplinary research. This opportunity will be an invaluable stepping stone toward achieving this goal, and I am confident that my background positions me well to contribute to the Institute's research community while benefiting immensely from its mentorship and training.

Thank you for your consideration. I look forward to the possibility of contributing to your research group.
